Sheep are domestic animals typically kept as livestock and raised for their wool, meat, and milk, and are occasionally used as model organisms in science. With over one billion domestic sheep in the world, they are the most numerous type of sheep, others being mountain sheep, snow sheep, and bighorn sheep, all of which are non-domestic sheep. An adult female is called a ewe, an adult male is called a ram, tup or wether, and juvenile sheep are called lambs.

A group of sheep is called a flock. Sheep are flocking animals, and this is one of the factors that lead to sheep being one of the first domesticated livestock species. Herding dogs are often used to herd sheep and direct them towards different pastures. (See flocking.)

The thing that sets sheep apart is their wool, which is sheared off and made into clothing, rugs, and upholstery. Different breeds produce different types of wool, which are used for different things. Some species of sheep, such as Merino sheep have very soft and fine wool, while others, like Iceland sheep, have thick, warm wool suitable for colder climates.
